### Sample Opening Vignette (Page 3)

**3:47 AM**

Sarah's phone pierces the darkness. Again.

"We have a situation," the night nurse says, her voice tight with stress.

It's the third call this week. Another fall. Another incident report. Another family that will be upset when they arrive in the morning.

Sarah is already calculating: Does she have enough staff to cover the increased monitoring? Will this trigger a state investigation? How will she explain this to the board?

She's been administrator of Riverside Manor for eight years. She loves her residents—knows their grandchildren's names, their favorite meals, the stories they tell about their youth. This is why she entered healthcare.

But lately, she wonders if she can keep doing this.

By 6:30 AM, she's at her desk, reviewing the incident report. The night shift ran with only two CNAs instead of four—two called out sick, and the agency had no one available. The remaining staff were stretched impossibly thin.

At 8:00 AM, Mrs. Henderson's daughter arrives, furious about her mother's medication being given two hours late yesterday. Sarah apologizes, explains the staffing shortage, promises it won't happen again. But she's not sure she can keep that promise.

At 9:00 AM, the agency calls: they have no temps available for tonight's shift either.

At 11:00 AM, the state survey team walks through the door, unannounced.

Sarah hasn't eaten. She hasn't seen a single resident today. She's been at her desk for four and a half hours, drowning in paperwork, phone calls, and crisis management.

This isn't why she became an administrator.

At 6:00 PM, she's still at her desk. Her own family has eaten dinner without her. Again.

Sarah loves her residents. She hates her job.

**This is the crisis facing residential care.**

And Sarah is not alone.
